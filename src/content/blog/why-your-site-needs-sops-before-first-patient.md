The pattern repeats at almost every new research site.

A site signs its first Clinical Trial Agreement. The PI is credentialed, the IRB approval comes through, and the coordinator starts screening patients. Everything feels like it's moving. Then the first monitoring visit happens.

The CRA asks for SOPs. The site coordinator pulls up a folder with two documents — one borrowed from a previous employer, one downloaded from a template site in 2019. Neither matches the current protocol. Neither references E6(R3). Neither has been signed by the current PI.

The monitor notes it as a finding. The sponsor flags it as a corrective action. Now the site is writing SOPs under pressure, during an active study, while also managing patients. This is how most sites operate, and it's avoidable.

## What a Monitoring Visit Actually Checks

A routine monitoring visit will verify that your site has SOPs covering, at minimum:

- **Training and qualification** of study staff (GCP, protocol-specific, DOA log)
- **Essential document management** (regulatory binder structure, filing conventions)
- **Informed consent** (all 21 CFR 50.25 elements, re-consent triggers)
- **Investigational product handling** (receipt, storage, dispensing, accountability)
- **Data management** (ALCOA+ principles, source documentation, CRF procedures)
- **Adverse event reporting** (timelines, SAE procedures, CAPA)
- **Quality control** (self-inspection, audit readiness)

If your site doesn't have documented procedures for these areas *before* enrollment begins, you're accumulating findings from day one — even if your actual operations are excellent. The gap isn't in what you do. It's in what you can prove you do.

## The Real Cost of Retroactive SOPs

Writing SOPs after a monitoring visit finding is expensive in three ways:

**Time.** SOPs written under CAPA pressure are rushed. A coordinator who should be managing patient visits is instead writing procedures. The turnaround expectation from sponsors is typically 30 days.

**Quality.** Reactive SOPs tend to be narrow — written to satisfy the specific finding rather than to serve as operational infrastructure. You end up with an SOP on "Adverse Event Reporting for Protocol XYZ" instead of a site-level SOP that covers your AE reporting process across all studies.

**Credibility.** Sponsors track findings. A site with SOP findings on its first monitoring visit signals operational immaturity. This matters when the same sponsor is deciding which sites get their next study.

## What "SOP-Ready" Looks Like

A compliance-ready site has 10-12 SOPs covering the full study lifecycle, each with:

- A clear scope and purpose statement
- Defined roles and responsibilities
- Step-by-step procedures with regulatory references
- Version control (version number, effective date, PI signature)
- A companion form or checklist where applicable

The key regulatory references are ICH E6(R2) — still the enforceable standard — with E6(R3) guidance notes where relevant. For US sites, the SOPs should reference 21 CFR Parts 50 (informed consent), 56 (IRBs), 312 (IND), and Part 11 (electronic records) as applicable.

This isn't aspirational. It's the baseline that sponsors expect when they award a study to your site.
