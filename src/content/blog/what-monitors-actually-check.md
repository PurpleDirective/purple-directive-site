Monitoring visits make coordinators nervous. They shouldn't — if your documentation is solid. The problem is that most sites confuse "doing good work" with "being audit-ready." They're related but not the same.

A monitor's job isn't to evaluate your clinical judgment. It's to verify that your documented processes match what actually happened. The gap between reality and documentation is where findings live.

## The Top 5 Findings We See Repeatedly

After working with multiple research sites across different therapeutic areas, the same issues surface over and over:

### 1. Informed Consent Timing Gaps

The consent form is signed, but the date on the consent doesn't align with the date of the first study procedure. Maybe screening labs were drawn the day before consent was documented. Maybe the subject signed at the visit but the coordinator didn't date-stamp until the next day.

The fix isn't complicated: document the consent process in real time. If there's a gap, document why. Monitors can work with explained gaps. They can't work with unexplained ones.

### 2. Delegation of Authority Log Gaps

The DOA log shows who is authorized to do what on the study. The common finding: someone performed a study procedure (blood draw, ECG, dispensing IP) who isn't listed on the DOA log, or was added after the fact.

Every person who touches the study in any capacity needs to be on the DOA log before they perform any study-related task. This includes sub-investigators, research nurses, and pharmacy staff.

### 3. Source Document Inconsistencies

The CRF says the subject's blood pressure was 128/82. The source document says 130/84. Which one is correct? Both were probably transcribed from the same reading, with a copying error in one direction.

ALCOA+ principles require that source data be attributable, legible, contemporaneous, original, and accurate. When the CRF and source disagree, the monitor has to query it. Multiply this by 50 data points per visit and you have a monitoring visit that takes twice as long as it should.

### 4. Protocol Deviation Documentation

Sites know when they've deviated from the protocol. What they often miss is the documentation: what happened, when it was identified, what corrective action was taken, and whether the IRB was notified within the required timeframe.

The deviation itself is usually minor. The documentation failure around it is what becomes a finding.

### 5. IP Accountability Gaps

Investigational product was dispensed, but the temperature log has a gap from the weekend. Or the drug accountability log shows 10 units received and 8 dispensed, but only 7 subjects have documented dosing records.

IP accountability is one of the areas where monitors have zero flexibility. Every unit must be accounted for, from receipt to return or destruction.

## The Pattern Behind the Findings

Every one of these is a documentation problem, not a clinical problem. The sites we work with are generally doing the right things — they're just not proving it consistently on paper.

The fix is systematic: SOPs that define the documentation requirements, forms that prompt for the right information at the right time, and a quality check process that catches gaps before the monitor does.

This is why we built our SOP library around documentation workflows, not clinical procedures. The clinical knowledge is already at the site. What's usually missing is the infrastructure that turns good practice into audit-ready evidence.
